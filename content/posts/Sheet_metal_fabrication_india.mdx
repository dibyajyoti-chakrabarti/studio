# Sheet Metal Fabrication in India: Processes, Design Rules & Cost Guide

Sheet metal fabrication is the process of forming flat metal sheets into functional components through cutting, bending, punching, welding, and finishing operations. It is one of the most widely used manufacturing processes in India — found in everything from electrical enclosures and HVAC ducting to automotive body panels and medical equipment cabinets.

For engineers and product teams ordering sheet metal parts in India, this guide covers every process step, the critical design rules that determine quality and cost, and how to choose the right fabrication partner.

---

## The Sheet Metal Fabrication Process: Step by Step

A typical sheet metal component goes through several sequential operations before it is ready for use. Understanding each step helps you design better parts and have informed conversations with your fabrication partner.

### Step 1: Material Procurement

Sheet metal fabrication begins with selecting and procuring the right raw material — coil or flat sheet — in the specified alloy, temper, and thickness. In India, standard sheet sizes are typically 1220mm × 2440mm (4ft × 8ft) or 1500mm × 3000mm (5ft × 10ft). Parts should be designed to nest efficiently within standard sheet dimensions to minimise scrap.

### Step 2: Cutting

The flat blank is cut to shape from the sheet. Cutting processes include:

- **Laser cutting** — High precision, excellent edge quality, suitable for 0.5mm–25mm thickness
- **Waterjet cutting** — No heat-affected zone, suitable for heat-sensitive materials and thick plate
- **Turret punching** — Fast for high-volume parts with regular hole patterns; uses hardened punch/die sets
- **Guillotine shearing** — Straight-line cuts only; fastest and cheapest for rectangular blanks

### Step 3: Forming (Bending)

Cut blanks are formed into 3D shapes using a **CNC press brake** — a machine that clamps the sheet between a punch and die to create precise, repeatable bends. Modern press brakes use back-gauges and angle-sensing technology to hold bend angles to ±0.5° tolerance consistently.

### Step 4: Fastener Insertion

Threaded fasteners — PEM nuts, studs, standoffs, and clinch nuts — are pressed into the sheet metal using a punch press or dedicated insertion tool. This creates strong, permanent threads in thin material that would not otherwise hold a machine screw reliably.

### Step 5: Welding and Assembly

Multiple formed components are joined by TIG, MIG, or spot welding to create assemblies. Sheet metal welding requires careful heat management to prevent distortion, particularly on thin (< 2mm) material.

### Step 6: Surface Finishing

Finished parts are cleaned, deburred, and processed through the required surface treatment — powder coating, anodising, zinc plating, or passivation — before final inspection and dispatch.

## Sheet Metal Materials and Properties

### Mild Steel (CRCA and HR)

Cold-rolled close-annealed (CRCA) mild steel is the most commonly fabricated sheet metal in India. It has excellent formability, welds cleanly, and takes powder coating and painting without pre-treatment issues. Hot-rolled (HR) steel is less expensive but has a rougher surface and poorer dimensional tolerances — suitable for structural work where appearance is secondary.

**Common thicknesses:** 0.8mm, 1mm, 1.2mm, 1.5mm, 2mm, 2.5mm, 3mm
**Best for:** Enclosures, panels, brackets, frames, chassis

### Stainless Steel

SS304 (18/8) is the workhorse of stainless fabrication — used in food processing equipment, pharmaceutical machinery, kitchen equipment, and architectural panels. SS316 adds molybdenum for superior chloride resistance, making it the choice for marine and chemical process environments.

**Formability:** Good, but springback is higher than mild steel — bend radii and K-factors must be adjusted
**Welding:** TIG welding preferred; MIG acceptable for structural applications

### Aluminium (5052 and 6061)

5052-H32 is the preferred alloy for sheet metal fabrication — it has excellent formability, good corrosion resistance, and welds well. 6061-T6 is stronger but less formable; it is better suited to machined or extruded components than sheet metal bending.

**Weight advantage:** 2.7 g/cm³ vs 7.85 g/cm³ for steel — approximately 3× lighter for the same volume
**Finishing:** Anodising provides excellent corrosion protection and a professional appearance

## Critical Design Rules for Sheet Metal

### Bend Radius

Every bend has a minimum inside radius determined by the material's ductility and thickness. Bending tighter than the minimum radius causes cracking on the outer surface of the bend.

**Rule of thumb:** Minimum inside bend radius = **material thickness × K-factor**

| Material | Recommended Min. Inside Radius |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Mild Steel (CRCA) | 0.5× thickness |
| Stainless Steel 304 | 1.0× thickness |
| Aluminium 5052-H32 | 0.5× thickness |
| Aluminium 6061-T6 | 3.0× thickness |

For a 2mm mild steel sheet, the minimum inside bend radius is 1mm. Always specify your bend radii on the drawing — if left unspecified, the shop applies a default that may not match your CAD model.

### Bend Relief

When a bend terminates near an edge or another bend, the material at the corner is subject to tearing stress. A **bend relief** — a small rectangular or circular notch cut at the bend terminus — prevents cracking and distortion.

```
Minimum bend relief depth   = material thickness + bend radius
Minimum bend relief width   = material thickness × 1.5
```

Omitting bend reliefs is one of the most common DFM mistakes on sheet metal parts. Every bend that runs into a perpendicular edge or another bend should have a bend relief unless the material is very thin (< 0.8mm).

### Hole-to-Bend Distance

Holes placed too close to a bend line deform when the sheet is bent — the material adjacent to the hole stretches, distorting the hole from round to oval.

**Minimum distance from hole edge to bend line:**

```
D_min = 2 × material thickness + bend radius
```

For a 2mm mild steel sheet with a 1mm bend radius:
`D_min = (2 × 2) + 1 = 5mm`

Any hole centre must be at least 5mm + hole_radius from the bend line.

### Minimum Flange Length

A flange is the flat section between a bend and the sheet edge. If the flange is too short, the press brake cannot grip the material to form the bend accurately.

**Minimum flange length = 4× material thickness + bend radius**, and no less than 3mm in absolute terms.

### K-Factor and Bend Allowance

When sheet metal is bent, the outer surface stretches and the inner surface compresses. The **neutral axis** — the line through the thickness that experiences neither tension nor compression — determines the correct **bend allowance**: how much flat material is consumed by each bend.

```typescript
// Bend allowance calculation
function bendAllowance(
  thickness: number,  // mm
  radius: number,     // inside radius, mm
  angle: number,      // bend angle in degrees
  kFactor: number     // typically 0.33–0.50
): number {
  const radians = (angle * Math.PI) / 180;
  return radians * (radius + kFactor * thickness);
}

// Example: 2mm CRCA, 1mm radius, 90° bend, K=0.42
const ba = bendAllowance(2, 1, 90, 0.42);
// ba = 1.571 × (1 + 0.42 × 2) = 2.926mm
```

Your CAD software handles this automatically if you set the correct K-factor for your material. Always verify the K-factor with your fabrication partner — different press brakes and tooling sets produce slightly different neutral axis positions.

## Common Sheet Metal Joins and Fastening Methods

### Welded Joints

Welding creates the strongest, most permanent joint between sheet metal components. TIG welding (GTAW) is preferred for visible joints, stainless steel, and thin material — it produces clean, controlled welds with minimal spatter. MIG welding (GMAW) is faster and more economical for structural mild steel work.

**Key consideration:** Welding distorts thin sheet metal. For panels thinner than 1.5mm, stitch welding (short welds with gaps) or spot welding reduces heat input and warping.

### PEM Fasteners (Press-In Nuts and Studs)

PEM fasteners — clinch nuts, self-clinching studs, and standoffs — are the industry standard for creating strong threads in sheet metal. They are pressed into a pre-punched hole and cold-formed into the sheet, creating a permanent fastener flush with the surface.

**When to use:** Any application where bolted assembly or repeated disassembly is required. Sheet thickness must be within the fastener's rated range (specified on PEM datasheets).

### Rivet Joints

Solid and blind (pop) rivets create permanent mechanical joints without welding. Blind rivets are particularly useful where access is limited to one side of the joint. Rivet joints are common in aerospace and HVAC fabrication.

### Hemmed Edges

A hem folds the sheet edge back on itself to create a smooth, rounded edge without a sharp burr — used on enclosure lids, appliance panels, and any surface that may be handled. Hems require a two-step bending operation (pre-hem and close-hem).

## Surface Finishes for Sheet Metal Parts

| Finish | Material | Protection | Appearance | Typical Cost |
|--------|----------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Powder Coating | Steel, Aluminium | Excellent | Matt/gloss, any RAL colour | Low–Medium |
| Anodising (Type II) | Aluminium only | Good | Satin, clear or coloured | Medium |
| Anodising (Type III / Hard) | Aluminium only | Excellent | Dark grey, very hard surface | Medium–High |
| Zinc Plating | Steel | Good | Bright silver or yellow chromate | Low |
| Hot-Dip Galvanising | Steel | Excellent | Spangle grey | Low (high volume) |
| Passivation | Stainless | Good | No visual change | Low |
| Electropolishing | Stainless | Excellent | Mirror bright | High |

**For most enclosures and panels in India:** Powder coating on CRCA mild steel is the optimal balance of protection, appearance, and cost. Specify RAL colour, gloss level (matt 30%, semi-gloss 50%, gloss 70%), and minimum dry film thickness (typically 60–80µm for indoor use, 80–120µm for outdoor).

## How to Reduce Sheet Metal Fabrication Costs

**1. Standardise material thickness across your assembly.** Every different thickness requires a separate tool setup on the press brake. An assembly using 1.5mm, 2mm, and 3mm parts costs more to produce than one standardised on 2mm throughout (where structurally acceptable).

**2. Design for standard bend angles.** 90° bends are fastest and cheapest. 45° and 135° bends require tool changes; very acute angles (< 30°) may require specialised tooling not available at every shop.

**3. Consolidate small parts into a single blank with living hinges or score-and-snap joints.** This reduces laser cutting setups, handling time, and assembly labour.

**4. Use standard fastener sizes.** M3, M4, M5, M6, and M8 PEM nuts and studs are stocked by every fabrication shop in India. Non-standard sizes incur procurement delays and premium pricing.

**5. Specify surface finish at design stage, not as an afterthought.** Finishing processes have DFM requirements — powder coating requires specific edge geometries for uniform film build; anodising requires all weld spatter to be removed before processing.
